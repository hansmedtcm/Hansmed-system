Stick out your tongue. What do you see? Most people see a pink, slightly coated surface and nothing more. A trained TCM practitioner sees something quite different — a detailed, dynamic map of your body's internal state, revealing patterns that can take years of symptoms to manifest in other ways.

Tongue examination (舌診, shé zhěn) has been a cornerstone of TCM diagnosis for over 2,000 years. Its principles were systematically documented in texts as early as the Shang Han Lun (傷寒論) during the Eastern Han Dynasty. Today, it remains one of the most objective and reproducible diagnostic tools in clinical TCM practice.

The tongue as a body map · 舌面臟腑分區

In TCM theory, different zones of the tongue correspond to different organ systems. This is not metaphorical — it is a clinically observed correspondence refined over centuries of practice.

TCM tongue map — organ system zones reflected on the tongue surface · 中醫舌面臟腑對應圖（僅供教育參考）

The tip of the tongue reflects the state of the Heart and Lung. Redness at the tip, for example, often accompanies stress, anxiety, or heat in the upper jiao. The centre of the tongue corresponds to the Spleen and Stomach — the digestive system's operational hub. The sides reflect Liver and Gallbladder function, and the root of the tongue — the area furthest back — maps to the Kidney system, which in TCM governs vitality, reproduction, and the body's fundamental reserves.

What a practitioner observes · 舌診的四個維度

TCM tongue examination systematically assesses four dimensions: colour, coating, shape, and moisture. Each dimension carries distinct diagnostic significance, and they are always read together — never in isolation.

General TCM tongue observation guide · For educational reference only · Not a diagnostic tool · 僅供教育參考，非診斷依據

Colour (舌色)

The body of the tongue — not the coating, but the tongue tissue itself — should ideally be a fresh, pale-red colour, sometimes described as the colour of fresh pork. Deviations from this norm carry meaning: a pale tongue often suggests blood or yang deficiency; a red or crimson tongue indicates heat; a purple or dark tongue points toward blood stagnation or cold.

Coating (舌苔)

The coating is produced by the digestive system and reflects the state of the stomach qi and the presence of pathogenic factors. A normal coating is thin and white. A thick yellow coating typically indicates heat or damp-heat in the digestive system. A thick white coating suggests cold or dampness. No coating at all — what practitioners call a "peeled" or "mirror" tongue — indicates yin deficiency or severe depletion of stomach fluids.

Shape (舌形)

A swollen or puffy tongue, particularly if it shows the imprint of the teeth along its edges (teeth marks, or 齒痕), indicates spleen qi deficiency — the spleen's failure to metabolise fluid efficiently. A thin or shrunken tongue points to blood or yin deficiency. Cracks in the tongue surface, especially running from front to back, typically indicate yin deficiency or chronic heat consuming body fluids.

Moisture (津液)

The tongue should be adequately moist — neither dry nor wet. A dry tongue suggests heat or yin deficiency depleting body fluids. An excessively wet or slippery tongue indicates coldness or an inability of the yang qi to transform fluids. This dimension is particularly important for assessing kidney yang function.

What tongue diagnosis cannot tell you · 舌診的局限性

Honesty is important here. Tongue examination is a valuable diagnostic tool, but it is not infallible and it does not operate in isolation. A single tongue observation provides a snapshot — not a full picture. Tongue characteristics can change from morning to evening, after eating or drinking, or in response to stress. Coffee and certain foods temporarily stain the coating. Medications can affect tongue colour and moisture.

An experienced practitioner reads the tongue alongside pulse, symptoms, and patient history. The tongue is one piece of a diagnostic picture — a significant one, but never the only one.
